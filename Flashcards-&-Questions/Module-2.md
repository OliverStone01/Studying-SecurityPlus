### Threat actors

<details>
  <summary>1. What is a threat actor?</summary>
  - The entity that causes the event that has an impact on the safety of another entity.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. What is another name for threat actor?</summary>
  - Malicious attacker
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. What information does knowing who the attacker is give you?</summary>
  - Why the attack is happening and whether its a directed or random attack
</details>


<details>
  <summary>4. Who could be an internal attacker?</summary>
  - An employee
</details>


<details>
  <summary>5. What are the other ways to characterise an attacker?</summary>
  - Resources, funding, and sophistication
</details>


<details>
  <summary>6. What are some motivations for attackers?</summary>
  - Data exfiltration, Espionage, Service disruption, Blackmail, Financial gain, Philosophical/political beliefs, Ethical, Revenge, Disruption/chaos, and war.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>7. What is a nation state hacker?</summary>
  - Hackers that are government run. These hackers have high amounts of funding and a high level of sophistication.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>8. Are nation state hackers internal or external?</summary>
  - External
</details>


<details>
  <summary>9. What might be the motivations of nation state hackers?</summary>
  - Data exfiltration, philosophical, revenge, disruption, and war.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>10. What does APT stand for?</summary>
  - Advanced persistent threat
</details>


<details>
  <summary>11. What is an unskilled hacker?</summary>
  - Attackers that run pre-made scripts with little knowledge on how they work. They are usually motivated by the attack it self. These attackers have little to no funding and low sophistication.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>12. What is a hactivist?</summary>
  - A hacker motivated by philosophical, revenge, disruption. These attackers can be highly sophisticated but funding may be limited.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>13. What is an insider threat?</summary>
  - Attackers that are inside of the company or organisation and are usually motivated by revenge. They also have a high level of resources being inside the company. They also have a medium level of sophistication.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>14. What is organised crime?</summary>
  - Criminals that are motivated by financial gain. These attackers are very sophisticated and organised.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>15. What is shadow IT</summary>
  - These are internal employees that go around IT and download software building their own infrastructure without concern for safety.
</details>

### Phishing

<details>
  <summary>1. What is something we can do to prevent being fooled by phishing?</summary>
  - Check the URL when on sites to make sure they are correct.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. What is spoofing?</summary>
  - The act of slightly changing something
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. What is typo squatting?</summary>
  - The act of spoofing the URL slightly in an attempt to lure in victims who do not realise.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>4. What is pretexting?</summary>
  - When attackers come up with a fake event in order to trick you into giving over sensitive information.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>5. What is vishing?</summary>
  - phishing over the phone
</details>


<details>
  <summary>6. What is smishing?</summary>
  - Phishing over text (SMS)
</details>

### Impersonation

<details>
  <summary>1. What is impersonation?</summary>
  - When an attacker pretends to be someone/something they are not.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. What is identity fraud?</summary>
  - When an attacker pretends to be you to steal your information or register something under your name.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. What are three things to prevent against impersonation?</summary>
  - Never volunteer information, Don’t disclose personal details, and always verify before revealing information.
</details>

### Watering hole

<details>
  <summary>1. What is a watering hole attack?</summary>
  - Where an attacker plants a trap on a third party site that the victim visits frequently.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. How can we protect against watering hole attacks?</summary>
  - We need a layered defence system and also make sure that firewalls and IPs are setup correctly.
</details>

### Memory injection

<details>
  <summary>1. Where must applications be for them to be run by the CPU?</summary>
  - In memory
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. Where must malware be to be able to run on the CPU</summary>
  - In memory
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. How can malware avoid detection?</summary>
  - By placing itself between existing processes
</details>


<details>
  <summary>4. What does injecting malware into an existing process to other than prevent detection?</summary>
  - Privilege escalation
</details>


<details>
  <summary>5. What does DLL Injection stand for?</summary>
  - Dynamic-Link Library
</details>


<details>
  <summary>6. Why does malware use a DLL Injection?</summary>
  - Other applications use this library so they can mask the detection better.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>7. How does a DLL Injection work?</summary>
  - The attacker will link the path to an infected DLL on a storage device as another application is running.
</details>

### Buffer overflow

<details>
  <summary>1. What is a buffer overflow attack?</summary>
  - When an attacker writes more than what is expected into an area of memory causing the excess data to be written into other areas.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. How can developers prevent this attack?</summary>
  - Using bounds checking
</details>

### Race conditions

<details>
  <summary>1. What is a race condition?</summary>
  - When multiple things are happening at the sane time.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. What is a common race condition attack?</summary>
  - Time-of-check to time-of-use (TOCTOU)
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. How does this attack work?</summary>
  - The attack changes the values between the time it takes for the system to check the value and update.
</details>

### Malicious updates

<details>
  <summary>1. What is a malicious update?</summary>
  - When their is malicious/vulnerable code written into an update
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. How do we prevent against malicious updates?</summary>
  - Keep known good backups to revert your system If the update is malicious.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>3. Where should you get your updates?</summary>
  - Reliable sources.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>4. Are automatics safe?</summary>
  - No, although they come from reliable sources, the update may still contain malicious code.
</details>

### Operating system vulnerabilities

<details>
  <summary>1. How important is it to update your systems?</summary>
  - Very. When windows release a security update, attacks try reverse engineer the vulnerabilities that the patches fix to be able to use the malicious code against non updated devices.
</details>


<details>
  <summary>2. Are these updates Safe?</summary>
  - Mostly, but it is still important to keep backups incase.
</details>
